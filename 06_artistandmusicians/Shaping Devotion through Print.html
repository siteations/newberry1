    Print facilitated an explosion of religious art but also facilitated its control and direction by religious and secular authorities. One particularly illuminating example of  centralized control involves the cult of Our Lady of Guadalupe, the most important center of devotion in the colony of New Spain.

    In the medieval church, such cults usually developed locally and spread by word of mouth, so the imagery, sites, and rituals associated with them were well-developed by the time the institutional church intervened. The veneration of the Virgin of Guadalupe, which began in earnest in the 17th century, took a much different course.

    Nearly everything about the cult symbolized the Christianization of the Americas: the official account records the Virgin appearing before Juan Diego, a Nahua convert, and the basilica that houses the miraculous image he received was built on a religious site once associated with the Aztec snake goddess Tonantzin. Visual imagery was essential to the development of the cult: from the Church’s perspective, the image of the Virgin was a powerful tool for replacing Tonantzin with Mary as the object of Indian devotion.

    This image, developed about the same time the printing press arrived in Mexico in the early 17th century, was standardized fairly quickly. Two works from the Newberry’s collection give a sense of the speed of this process. The earlier Garden of Poetry in honor, praise, and obedience…to the most-blessed Virgin includes the iconic image of the Virgin (featuring a robe, crown, and angel) alongside traces of the Aztec cult the Virgin subsumed, such as the mountain of Tepeyec, where Tonantzin was worshiped. Just about two decades later, The Star of Northern Mexico demonstrated how the standard iconography for the Virgin was firmly in place, this time without any visual reference to any pre-Christian religious roots.
