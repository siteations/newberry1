    In the medieval church, sacred music was primarily heard inchurches or other religious institutions. Although religious themes did find their way into secular music, there remained an ideal separation between the two. This distinction partially collapsed in the early modern period, thanks in large part to print.

    As lay religious participation expanded, sacred music was increasingly found outside of religious institutions. Laypeople were eager to hear and perform spiritual songs, psalmody, and hymns at home, so they bought books like William Byrd’s Psalmes, Sonets, & Songs of Sadnes and Pietie, which included arrangements of sacred music for multiple voices and instruments. In this way, lay musicians could experience all sacred music outside of church.  Indeed, Byrd assured his readers that his collection would help them prepare them to pray, praise God when they felt “merrie,” and “lament for thy sins.”

    As different musical traditions developed among the different churches, worshippers worried that their spiritual life would suffer if they did not have access to the hymns, songs, and liturgical music they knew best. To alleviate this concern, printers produced portable music books that allowed worshipers to have their particular brand of sacred music with them at all times. The small Spiritual Songs and Hymns for all Sundays and Feast Days, which contained the lyrics for numerous Protestant hymns, was clearly marketed to users who sought assurance that they would never lack the spiritual aid, whether at home or travelling.

    When necessary, religious authorities were willing to co-opt secular music to achieve their spiritual goals. They did so by setting religious lyrics to the tunes of popular songs (in a process called “contrafaction”) so that entertaining secular melodies could do spiritual work. Catholics in France, for instance, were deeply concerned about the Protestant dominance of the printing market (and thus public opinion), and produced The Pious Alouette as a musical attempt to turn the tide. The new songs it contained used popular melodies to broadcast Catholic theology and practice; the example here provides a catchy overview of Catholic Eucharistic theology, in the hopes of making it more appealing  to a general audience.
