    Christianity had long utilized visual art. Medieval church leaders believed that teaching the more complex and paradoxical doctrines of the faith required visual representation of some sort, and churches were filled with them. Paintings, altarpieces, sculpture, stained glass, tapestries, and more were all used to tell the Christian story to a general audience and support the process of devotion.
    From the 1520s, however, organized efforts to remove imagery and decorative objects from churches, alongside popular outbursts of iconoclasm, changed the visual character or thousands of European places of worship.  In some cases, church-based imagery was hidden an later reappeared, but in other cases it was lost forever.
    Notwithstanding early modern iconoclasm, art proved no less important to religion in this modern period than before. Many people looked to visual art to help make sense of the religious changes overturning much of their lives. The Character of Religion, for instance, expresses well the pervasive religious anxiety of the period. This large print shows a ship making its way towards the harbor of salvation through treacherous seas in which damned sinners are consumed by monsters. Although the print reveals an obvious Catholic perspective, its chaotic depiction of religion and the pursuit of salvation offers a fairly accurate description of how many early modern Christians experienced religious change.
    For centuries, visual art had taught ordinary, illiterate worshipers the stories from Scripture, the lives of the saints, and important doctrines. Artists were called upon to depict aspects of Christianity that defied ready verbal description, so that a broader audience could appreciate them. The book of Revelation (also known as the Apocalypse), the final canonical book of the Christian Bible, was a great source for such interpretive art, since it is filled with dense and puzzling prophetic language Its visual representation remained important in the era of print culture, as attested not only by Luther’s German New Testament but also by The Apocalypse of St. John, a 15th-century block book that illustrated John’s terrifying and awe-inspiring visions of the end times.
    Early modern artists continued to find steady work producing imagery of saints, Christ, and Christian religious themes. Thanks to print, such devotional images now became much more widely available than in the medieval period. Many taught viewers how to use devotional imagery by providing a focus for contemplation. This engraving from The Life of the Seraphic Mother Saint Teresa is a typical example. It shows how St. Teresa of Ávila “learned to do great things” by reading the work of St. Gregory the Great, and by gazing at the image of the great saint on her wall. Readers of this book were thereby encouraged to use this image and the account of St. Teresa’s life in precisely the same way.
