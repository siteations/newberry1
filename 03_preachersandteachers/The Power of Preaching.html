    The Power of Preaching

    1 2017-06-20T19:38:37+00:00 Newberry Library 09980eb76a145ec4f3814f3b9fb45f381b3d1f02 3 5 plain 2017-06-30T18:31:33+00:00 Newberry Library 09980eb76a145ec4f3814f3b9fb45f381b3d1f02
    It is no accident that many of the most significant agents of early modern religious change were accomplished preachers. Such men were deeply familiar with the means of uniting rhetoric and theology effectively to instill religious doctrines and godly behavior in the hearts and minds of an audience. As Christianity splintered during the 16th century, these skills became necessary for protecting and promoting the competing visions of “true” Christianity and were deployed to larger audiences.
    No one in the medieval church engaged with the people more directly, publicly, or frequently than the preachers. Their sermons were always presented before large gatherings, either in worship or in other public areas. Moreover, sermons were often copied and distributed widely so they could be read or preached again. In fact, sermons remained one of the most important elements of the printing business, offering the public – and the clergy themselves – crucial opportunities for spiritual guidance and rhetorical emulation.
    The manuscript shown here, Tract…on the Tribulations Resulting from the Defective Administration of the Church, an excerpt from a sermon delivered by the famed Paris theologian Jean Gerson on the need to reform the church, helps to explain the expanding importance of the sermon, even before printing. The small size, less formal style, and more direct argumentation of sermons like this one clearly influenced the development of the early modern pamphlet.
    Skilled preachers could command tremendous political and social influence. As lay interest in religious matters grew, men (and even some women) who had mastered the art of preaching were able to convince large numbers of people to change their behavior…sometimes in ways authorities did not approve. The Little Book and Legal Action against Brother Girolamo the Heresiarch stands as a potent reminder of the preacher’s power. The work is a refutation of Girolamo Savonarola, who through his preaching achieved renown and great influence in Florence after the expulsion of the Medici in 1494, charismatically leading a program of austere religious reform that angered government officials and the papacy. Though he was eventually executed and, as the woodcut shows, rebranded as a demonically possessed figure, Savonarola’s spectacular success still spoke as a cautionary tale of the tremendous social power of effective preaching.
