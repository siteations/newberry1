    Preachers and Teachers (2/4)

    Early Modern Preaching Aids

    1 2017-06-20T19:40:50+00:00 Newberry Library 09980eb76a145ec4f3814f3b9fb45f381b3d1f02 3 4 plain 2017-06-30T18:36:59+00:00 Newberry Library 09980eb76a145ec4f3814f3b9fb45f381b3d1f02
    The importance of preaching only grew during the early modern period. New churches depended on effective preaching to establish themselves and their ideas, while older ones used sermons to reassert the validity of their theology, doctrine, and rites. All sides recognized preachers as key advocates in their territories and often beyond, and the demand for more and better preachers created an equally important demand for better resources to train them.
    Early modern preaching aids -- especially manuals teaching the basic rhetorical tools for successful preaching -- often reflected the more complicated world in which preachers worked. The Americas in particular offered a daunting challenge; there, preachers played an important part in converting native peoples to Christianity, and often in attempting to expunge traditional religious practices.  Likewise, their rhetorical efforts sometimes had to guard against the incursion of other forms of Christianity.  And all the while, they had to confront cultures and languages they did not understand well, or at all.

    Diego Valadés, a native of New Spain, experienced these challenges firsthand, as recorded in a treatise on the art of preaching titled Christian Rhetoric. Valadés incorporated descriptions of indigenous culture and indigenous imagery to illustrate the traditional rhetorical strategies for good preaching. He also included detailed accounts of the ways in which Europeans could preach effectively to Indian audiences; the image shown here depicts the common Franciscan practice of using visual representations (in this case, of Christ’s Passion) to explain the faith to new converts almost none of whom could read European languages.
    Other early modern preaching aids reflected the interest in controlling preaching as much as possible. This was especially prevalent in Protestant lands, where the sermon had become the central moment of the worship service. The guarantee of a weekly audience presented an opportunity to instill doctrines and behaviors into a potentially skeptical audience, provided that the preachers remained on message. This would be more easily done if the preachers stuck to a particular script, and many preaching aids provided exactly that.

    In England, the pulpit had been used with some success to support the Reformation as soon as Henry VIII broke from Rome, but even better results were expected with the publication of Certain Sermons to Be Read in Churches, also known as the Book of Homilies. This work, which evolved across the decades, contained state- and church-sanctioned sermons that were required to be read in all parish churches, often on particular Sundays. In this way, the crown and church hoped to teach the essentials of Protestant Christianity to the people, establish the theological standard by which any doctrinal idea was to be judged, form England into a truly godly society, and generate support for the Tudor regime in power.
