    1. 1 2017-06-14T20:21:14+00:00 Active Religious Resistance 10 plain 2017-08-15T19:55:57+00:00
      Princes had good reason to be wary of their people in the early modern world. After Luther challenged the Roman church and survived, the authority of princes did not seem quite as unassailable as it once did. Accordingly, more and more subjects were willing to stand up to their princes if they felt God was on their side.

      Print gave a voice to the people that could be very effective in bringing princes into line when they threatened “true” Christianity. The fiery minister and reformer John Knox, for instance, was concerned that the Church of Scotland was slipping back into “the poisoned cup of Rome,” and he sent a letter on the matter to Mary of Guise, the Queen Regent. When she failed to take his letter seriously, he published the longer and more forceful Copie of a Lettre Delivered to the Ladie Marie, which increased public pressure on Mary to reform the Scottish church or suffer God’s wrathful judgment.
      Religion could also lead the people into open rebellion. This was clear from the early days of the Protestant Reformation thanks to the Peasants’ War of 1524-1525, which raged mostly in southwestern and central Germany. Unlike previous uprisings, these rebellious peasants claimed to act primarily on religious convictions, citing the Gospel to justify their revolt. The peasants’ demands included religious matters like the ability to choose their own pastors alongside more routine economic or legal points of contention, such as an end to excessive taxation. This new use of the Gospel for political purposes alarmed Martin Luther, who leaped into the fray by writing this pamphlet, Admonition to Peace in Response to the Twelve Articles of the Peasantry in Swabia. Though he was sympathetic to their plight, Luther insisted that the peasants abused the Gospel by using it to secure material gains rather than salvation. Indeed, he did not even bother to respond in detail to the non-religious demands, categorically dismissing them all in the section shown here.
      The Peasants’ War did not lead to any substantial change in Germany at the time, but it foreshadowed the devastating consequences that would come in later decades when the religious convictions of the princes and their people were at odds. Therein lay the new problem in the prince-people dynamic for the entirety of our period: both sides wanted peace, but few were willing to sacrifice “true” Christianity or their power in order to achieve it.
