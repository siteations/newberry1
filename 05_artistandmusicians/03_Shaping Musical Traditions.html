    Shaping Musical Traditions

    Early modern people treasured the sacred music that set their sect apart. Their relationship with it was personal and subjective, shaped by the ways in which music met their spiritual needs. Religious communities actively shaped their musical lives in ways that best accommodated their understanding of Christianity.

    Although Protestant churches operated with less hierarchical structure than the Catholic Church, making them more able to adapt their own liturgical traditions, many congregations used this autonomy precisely to preserve musical traditions from the medieval church. We can see this flexibility in the pages of Latin Ecclesiastical Songs, a liturgical handbook used at a Lutheran Latin school. The manuscript additions shown here are tropes (new music created to frame old Gregorian chants) on the Kyrie, the first part of the mass. Tropes were very popular in the Middle Ages, and this book illustrates that Lutheran institutions had no problem preserving them in their new church.

    Despite Catholic efforts to ban such tropes, institutions continued used them for centuries, as this Gradual from a Mexican convent, most likely made during the early 18th century, shows. A similar story is told by this Mexican Choir Book, which contains polyphonic masses and motets composed for a convent in Mexico, where they were performed by nuns. The Council of Trent had seriously considered banning such music in convents in the 1560s, but the matter was ultimately dropped, as the council realized there was only so much they could do to control the performance of sacred music.
