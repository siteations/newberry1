    Whatever church they belonged to, most early modern Christians, from elites to ordinary people, encountered music at worship and often in their homes. But each church used music in its own way, tuned to its sense of the spiritual needs of its members.

    Luther’s modern fame rests as much on his lyrics as on his pamphlets or his Bible. For Lutherans, music was a glue binding congregations together and a source of spiritual strength. Martin Luther believed that singing sacred music elicited a great joy that was powerful enough to drive the devil away, and he ensured that music would be an essential part of his church. Luther composed hymns reinforcing key doctrines such as the assurance of divine grace and justification through faith alone. His most famous hymn, “A Mighty Fortress” (shown here in a late 16th-century edition of Spiritual Songs), reminded the faithful that the devil could do nothing to take God’s grace away from them. Congregational singing immediately became a hallmark of Lutheran worship, remaining so to this day.

    Music held a much less prominent place in the Reformed church. John Calvin was skeptical of the spiritual value of music, since he feared delightful melodies could seduce believers away from focusing on God. In the end, however, even he grudgingly allowed music in Reformed worship, provided that it kept worshippers’ focus strictly on Scripture. For this reason, the singing of the Psalms (psalmody) became the most important musical component of the early Reformed Church and even for their soldiers preparing for battle. Notated Psalters such as the Psalms of David set these ancient texts to new melodies that were intended to breathe new life into worshippers’ understanding of God and his commandments.

    For Catholics, music remained a vital part of liturgy and devotion, as it had been for centuries. In the early modern period, however, Catholic musicians and authorities also used it to reinforce distinctive aspects of Catholic identity in the midst of a bitterly divided Christianity. The opera The Tragedy of St. Boniface, for instance, used the martyrdom of a famous early Christian as a pretext for commenting on the central place of martyrdom (still a very real possibility for Catholic missionaries working in Britain or Japan.)
